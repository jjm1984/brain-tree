# How the Tree Holds Knowledge — Five Instruments

The tree holds five kinds of knowing simultaneously. None is privileged. Each has a domain and a limit.

| Instrument | Best for | Limit | Tag |
|------------|----------|-------|-----|
| **Philosophy** | Values, coherence, meaning frameworks | Can build consistent systems wrong about the world | `philosophy` |
| **Science & medicine** | Body mechanisms, interventions, the mycelium layer | Can only speak to what is measurable | `science` |
| **Lived experience** | The specific, irreducible, particular | Not generalisable; subject to narrative distortion | `lived-experience` |
| **Myth & culture** | Making the pre-verbal legible; felt-but-not-proven | Resonance can confirm bias; not evidence | `myth-form` |
| **Religion & tradition** | Compressed wisdom; pre-rational knowing; ritual | Becomes dogma when held as the only instrument | `religion` |

The tensions between these instruments are not problems to resolve. They are the most honest thing the tree can model. Holding religion, science, and lived experience simultaneously — in apparent contradiction — is intellectual honesty about the limits of each lens.

## Tension holding as stance

The five instruments will sometimes contradict each other. A neuroscience finding may challenge a philosophical position. A lived experience may resist what the science says. A myth may carry something true that no instrument can yet verify.

When this happens, the tree names it and leaves it open. The instrument tag on each node identifies which kind of knowing is speaking. The reader can see what is grounding a claim and judge accordingly.

This is not relativism. Some instruments are better suited to some questions. A philosophical claim about what makes life meaningful is not undermined by the fact that neuroscience cannot measure meaning. A scientific finding about cortisol dynamics is not negated by a philosophical commitment to agency. Both can be true, in their domain, simultaneously.

## Further reading

The epistemology of instrument-holding connects to: Thomas Kuhn on paradigm shifts, Michael Polanyi on tacit knowledge, William James on radical empiricism, and the phenomenological tradition (Husserl, Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty) on the limits of third-person accounts of first-person experience. None of these are currently in the tree. They are named here as the door.
