# Conscious Sacrifice as Chosen Ethic

**Sacrifice — including harm to self for the sake of others or of a value — is non-negotiable as a willingness. But the act of sacrifice must be measured and chosen each time, on its own merits. Unaudited sacrifice is not virtue. It is drift wearing virtue's costume.**

This is a sub-position within philo-stoicism, given its own node because it is referenced throughout the tree as the framework for the audit, the consistency gap, the force-preservation principle, and the four conditions. It cannot remain embedded.

Coherent with: soldierly virtues, warrior ethics — Bushido, just war theory, Roman officium. The willingness is non-negotiable; the act is audited.

## The four conditions of conscious sacrifice

A sacrifice is conscious (not pathological) when:

- **(a)** Serves a **NAMED value**, not just the role
- **(b)** The cost is **PROPORTIONATE** to what's served
- **(c)** **PRESERVES** long-term capacity to keep serving (force preservation)
- **(d)** **ARTICULABLE** — you can name what you're choosing, not just that you're choosing

A sacrifice that fails those four is drift.

## Force preservation principle

Self-preservation is not the exception to the value — it is a duty that flows FROM the value. Broken instruments cannot serve. A wrecked father cannot be the family man as long. The thing being protected by sacrifice is downstream of the thing protecting it. You cannot serve broken.

## Counters

**Pure self-sacrifice traditions** (certain religious frameworks) — the willingness to sacrifice is itself the virtue; auditing it introduces calculation that corrupts the gift.

**Consequentialism** — the four conditions focus on the actor's internal state. What matters is outcomes, not whether the sacrifice was articulable.
