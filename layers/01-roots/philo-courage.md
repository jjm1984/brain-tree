# Courage — Three Kinds, One Capacity

**Courage lives within you and only shows itself at the hard moments. Sometimes you aren't aware of it until circumstances call it forth. Difficulty is not an excuse — the moral choice is still yours to make.**

The family philosophy: *Some things are hard. In our family we do it anyway.*

## The origin stories

**The river** — age 6. Youngest sister, aged 3, floating down the Mary River. Without considering whether he could, ran and dived from a great height, swam until he reached her, dragged her back upstream — nearly passing out as he came ashore. His parents never saw it.

**The snake** — age 7. Middle sister screamed snake. Ran fifty metres toward it — to place himself between her and the danger.

Both moments happened before any philosophy of courage existed. Done in private, without witness, without reward. A person who learns early that courage is done without applause carries that differently than someone whose bravery was celebrated.

## Bravery vs Courage

**Bravery**: acute response to immediate physical threat. Adrenaline-driven. Instinctive. Doesn't require values or choice — requires nerve and a nervous system that doesn't freeze. You can be brave without being courageous.

**Courage**: more deliberate. Requires awareness of the cost, and the choice to proceed anyway. Can be quiet. Can be slow. Doesn't need adrenaline.

The river and the snake weren't just bravery — they were bravery directed by value. The value of others over self. That is what elevated them from instinct to something more.

## Three kinds

**Physical** — the body moving before the mind catches up. The easiest kind — it bypasses choice. Built through character long before the moment of crisis.

**Moral** — standing for a person or idea against wrong thinking or behaviour. The standard you ignore is the standard you accept. Harder — requires choosing discomfort publicly, with consequences.

**Grit** — showing up. Every day. Getting out of bed. Being consistent when nothing dramatic is at stake, when no one is watching, when the reward is distant or invisible. The hardest kind — no adrenaline, no snake, no river. Just today, again.

## What courage feeds

- **Truth** — courage is what makes you say it when it costs you.
- **Kindness** — courage is what stops kindness from collapsing into protective silence. Without courage, kindness becomes a way of hiding.
- **Meaning** — courage is what keeps the motion honest when fear wants to soften it.
- **Accountability** — courage is what makes you look at what you did — or didn't do — without flinching.
- **Forgiveness** — courage is what makes you walk the arc of harm honestly rather than bypassing the grief.

## What feeds courage (open question)

Heroic courage appears to be fed by the value of others over self. But grit courage — showing up for yourself, daily, when no one needs saving — may require something different. Is it self-worth? The belief that you matter enough to show up for? Planted as a question, not answered.

## Crossroads

**Is courage the absence of fear — or action despite it?**

Path A: In physical courage, there was no fear — the body moved before fear could form. Courage may sometimes be the temporary suspension of the fear mechanism, not its override. Some courage is pre-cognitive. Character moving faster than thought.

Path B: Moral and grit courage are chosen in full awareness of the cost. Fear is present. The choosing happens anyway. Most courage is not the absence of fear. It is action that fear cannot stop.

*The two childhood stories suggest physical courage may be a different category — pre-deliberate, character-driven. The harder question is whether that kind of courage can be trained, or whether it simply reveals what was already there.*

**Does courage require a witness — or is it complete in private?**

Path A: Both childhood moments happened without witness. The parents never saw the river. Courage is complete in itself, regardless of whether it is seen. Courage that requires an audience may not be courage. It may be performance.

Path B: Moral courage — standing against wrong thinking or behaviour — necessarily occurs in public. The witness is part of the act.

*The three kinds of courage may have different relationships to witness. Physical: private. Moral: public. Grit: both, and neither.*

## Open threads

- What feeds courage — what sustains it across time when the adrenaline is gone and the cause is distant?
- Is self-worth the fuel of grit courage — and does the consistency gap reveal a self-worth problem in disguise?
- Can grit courage coexist with grace — the permission to not show up sometimes — or does the discipline require absoluteness?
- Is the family philosophy building courage in children — or a relationship with difficulty that needs examining for its shadow?

## Counters

**Aristotle** — courage as the mean between cowardice and recklessness. Not all risk-taking is courage. Not all restraint is cowardice. `yellow partially resonant`

**Pure pacifism** — physical courage in service of violence is not virtue. The capacity for violence, however directed, is morally suspect.

**Recklessness** — the river and the snake could have ended differently. The body that moves before the mind catches up is also the body that drowns. Instinctive courage without wisdom is danger wearing heroism's costume.

## Exposed roots

- **Aristotle — courage as the mean** `yellow partially resonant`
- **Stoicism — the discipline of the will** — the Stoics trained for adversity in small things so the large moments would find them ready. Grit courage is directly Stoic in its texture. `yellow present in the training philosophy`
- **Biblical — David, Samson, Daniel** — physical and moral courage in the face of overwhelming force. Used as imaginative reference points, not doctrine. `yellow alive as image`
- **Nelson Mandela, Viktor Frankl** — grit courage under sustained pressure. Showing up over years and decades when the cause seems lost. `peripheral, not yet explored directly`
