# How to Live Well

**Do what is fulfilling — but cause no harm.**

This is the trunk. The tension between fulfilment and harm-avoidance is not a problem to resolve — it is the engine of good decisions. Everything else in the tree runs through this.

The statement is deceptively simple. It does not resolve itself. There are moments when what is fulfilling causes harm, and moments when avoiding harm costs fulfilment. Those moments are the actual territory of the philosophy. The trunk does not provide an escape from that tension. It provides an orientation.

## The harm-to-self question (answered 2026-05-31)

Harm to self is an acceptable price for no-harm-to-others, AND is an acceptable price for doing the value — for some things. Coherent with the soldierly virtues: protecting others is more worthy than protecting yourself. But this is held as a chosen ethic, not an automatic one. Each instance must be audited on its own merits to avoid the cycle of sacrifice-for-sacrifice's-sake. See the self-worth branch: the audit framework and the four conditions of conscious sacrifice.

## Open threads

- When fulfilment and harm-avoidance conflict directly, what is the tiebreaker?
- If misunderstanding someone is a form of harm, what does that demand of me as a parent, leader, partner?

## Counters

**Asceticism** (Buddhist, Stoic strands) — fulfilment is a trap. Desire is the source of suffering. Discipline and renunciation, not pursuit, is the path.

**Pure altruism** (Bentham, Singer) — your own fulfilment is morally irrelevant. What matters is the greatest good for the greatest number.

**Moral absolutism** — 'cause no harm' is too vague without codification. Harm is defined by fixed rules, not context.

## Exposed roots

- **Aristotle — Eudaimonia** — the flourishing life as the aim. Resonant but not identical: eudaimonia is more structured than this position. `yellow partial`
- **Existentialism (Sartre)** — the self defines its own good. Resonant in the emphasis on chosen ethics, not given ones. `yellow partial`
- **Care ethics (Noddings, Gilligan)** — harm understood relationally, not abstractly. `peripheral`

## Dear reader

The trunk is not a conclusion. It is a starting point that generates questions. If you find it too simple, you are reading it correctly — the work is in the application, not the statement.
