# Musculature — Capability Made Physical

Musculature is different from the other four systems. Brain, nervous system, hormones, and nutrition are infrastructure — they enable or constrain. Musculature is capability made physical. It is the body prepared for action not yet named. This is where philosophy crystallises into physical form — the character that moved toward the snake already existed in the tissue before the moment called for it.

## Three expressions

**Trained reflexes** — movements before thought. The swim. The snake. Courage expressed as muscle memory, not decision. The body executing what character built before the crisis arrived. Merleau-Ponty's point made physical: perception and action are not separate. The body that has trained reaches and understands simultaneously.

The inner witness at its most pre-cognitive — present but pre-verbal. The river and the snake happened before courage was a concept. The musculature was the philosophy before the philosophy existed.

**The hero arc** — capability built without knowing what it will be needed for. Training in peacetime. Building the physical capacity to respond when the moment arrives without knowing what the moment will be. The Stoic practice of voluntary hardship — not suffering for its own sake, but building the substrate of response.

The hero arc is the physical expression of the force-preservation principle — not preserving current capacity, but building future capacity. The opposite of the worth-machine's self-depletion pattern.

**Planned capability** — the athlete who knows the event and trains toward it. Purposeful, directional, outcome-oriented. Different from the hero arc in that the goal is named. Related in that both require sustained investment in the physical substrate before it is demanded. Connects to 1% compounding — the daily anchor that becomes, over time, the capability that was not there before.

## Open question

What is the body currently being prepared for? The question is not rhetorical — the musculature system needs a named direction to train toward. The force-preservation principle implies this should be explicit.
