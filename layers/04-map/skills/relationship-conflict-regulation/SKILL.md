# Understanding and Responding to Relationship Conflict

A skill for the moment something small blows up bigger than it should —
either yours or someone else's.

## When to use this

- A small comment lands as a much bigger deal than intended.
- Someone goes quiet, leaves the room, or shuts down mid-conversation.
- You notice yourself doing the same thing.
- Something old seems to be firing through something new.

## Understand first (read this before you need it, not during)

Reactions like this are usually not a choice made in the moment. The
alarm goes off before the thinking part of the brain gets a vote —
that's true of everyone's nervous system, not a flaw specific to one
person. This doesn't make the impact of the reaction disappear, and it
doesn't mean nothing is anyone's responsibility. It means the fair
question is asked *after* the alarm has been named, not during it.

Capacity has to be checked before character does. If someone's
nervous system has already gone off, you're not looking at a
decision — you're looking at a structural-capacity event. Whether
they own their part comes later, once capacity is back online.

## The sequence, in order

**1. Recognise the shape.**
Most conflict has a pursuer and a withdrawer. One person moves toward
the issue, the other moves away from it. Neither role is more at
fault — the shape itself is the first thing to notice, before
assigning blame to either side of it.

**2. Regulate.**
Name to yourself, out loud if you can, what's happening in your body
right now. This is the step that's most likely to get skipped, and
skipping it is what turns a reaction into an escalation.

**3. Borrow peace — pick one:**

  - *Indifferent-order peace* — step away to something that asks
    nothing of you back: a walk, an instrument, quiet. Use this when
    the flood is too big for another person to be useful right now.
  - *Co-regulation* — stay close to someone steadier and let their
    calm do some of the work. Use this when what's actually needed is
    connection, not distance.

  **How to tell which one you need — the honest answer is you often
  can't tell in the moment.** If you're not sure, default to a short,
  pre-agreed signal rather than guessing: say you need a minute, and
  say you'll be back. That signal is what keeps space from reading as
  abandonment.

**4. Inner witness — notice, don't diagnose.**
Once regulation has started, get curious about what actually fired.
Was it the words themselves, or something the words touched? This is
an observation to hold loosely, not a label to apply to someone else
from the outside. See full reference for a note on this.

**5. Come back together, against the problem, not each other.**
Once both people have capacity again, return to what actually
happened — as two people looking at the same problem, not two people
across from each other.

## What this skill is not

Not a script for winning an argument. Not a diagnosis of the other
person. Not permission to skip accountability once capacity has
returned. It's a shared map for the ten minutes where nobody in the
room has full access to their own thinking yet.
