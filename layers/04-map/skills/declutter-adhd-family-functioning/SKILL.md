# Reducing Clutter-Related Decision Fatigue in an ADHD Household

A skill for the moment of deciding what to keep — not a checklist of items to throw out.

## When to use this

- Facing a pile, drawer, or room that needs sorting and the sorting itself feels exhausting before it starts.
- Building a family declutter routine and want it to actually stick.
- Noticing the same categories of stuff piling up again after a previous clear-out.

## The decision procedure (apply per item, not per room)

**1. Zero-decision discard.** Items with no possible use-case ambiguity — expired food, broken containers, unmatched socks or lids, worn-through items. Bin immediately. No "maybe" pile.

**2. One-place-only items.** Anything with a single designated home — keys, chargers, paperwork. If it doesn't have one, decide the place now, not later.

**3. Duplicate-of-a-kept-item.** Excess mugs, bags, hangers, pens beyond a set number. Keep a fixed cap, discard the rest without re-evaluating each one individually.

**4. Deferred-decision items.** Sentimental or ambiguous-value items get ONE deferred box with a review date — not indefinite deferral. Postponement needs a forcing mechanism or it silently becomes never.

## Tactic note

The point is minimizing the *number of individual decisions required*, not the amount of clutter. Decision volume — not clutter volume — is the actual ADHD-relevant cost. A flat "25 items to get rid of" checklist doesn't reduce that load; the sorting rule does.

## What this skill is not

Not a one-time purge. Not a judgment about what's worth keeping. It's a rule for making the deciding itself cheaper, repeatable across rooms and over time.
