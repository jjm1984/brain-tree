# Borrowed Peace — Co-Regulation

Peace borrowed not from something indifferent, but from a regulated partner extending regulation to one who isn't. Mechanism differs from indifferent-order peace precisely where it matters: indifferent peace is free to the giver; co-regulation is not.

## Crossroads: Co-regulation or space?

Which the moment needs isn't a fixed trait of the person ("she's a co-regulation person"). Per the flight/freeze framing already established in `leaf-withdrawal-repair-pattern`, where it sits is determined by how much self-awareness is available in the moment — which pushes the answer back to inner witness as the thing that has to run before this fork can even be read correctly.

**Path A — Co-regulation.** The regulated partner extends steadiness across; proximity itself is the repair mechanism. Right when the trigger is manageable-but-present and connection is what's actually being sought.

**Path B — Space.** Regulate-alone first, signal that regulation is underway (the same clause already named as `leaf-withdrawal-repair-pattern` step 5), reconnect after. Right when the nervous system is too flooded for co-regulation to land as anything but more input.

**Dear reader:** not a fixed trait of the person — where this sits shifts moment to moment with how much self-awareness is actually available.

## Posture as a receiver-side signal — dominance perception (distinct from power-posing)

Separate from and more robust than the power-posing/Cuddy line of research (see the rejected-claim note directly below), a longer-standing body of work on how posture is *perceived by others* — not how it changes the poser's own chemistry — is directly relevant here. Expansive, space-taking posture reads as dominant; contractive, small posture reads as submissive — a consistent, cross-species finding (Argyle 1988; Burgoon & Dunbar 2006). More specifically load-bearing: Tiedens & Fragale (2003) found that complementarity, not mirroring, produces comfort — when one person displays a dominant/expansive posture (which a dysregulated, activated nervous system effectively does), a partner who deliberately takes the smaller, lower, less expansive posture in response produces more comfort and liking than a partner who matches or escalates. This is not simply "calm is contagious" — it specifically means *relative* size/expansiveness is what's being read, which is exactly what "getting on their level, side-on, lower" does structurally: it manufactures a favourable relative-size signal on purpose, not just a generically calm one.

No counter needed for this specific claim — a distinct research tradition from power-posing, well-replicated, not part of the psychology replication crisis that affected the hormonal power-posing claims.

**Investigated and explicitly rejected — record kept so this isn't re-investigated:** the claim that adopting a posture changes the *adopter's own* hormones (testosterone/cortisol) — the Amy Cuddy "power posing" claim — did not survive replication. Multiple large-sample replications reproduced the self-reported subjective-feeling effect but not the hormonal effect, and co-author Dana Carney later published a statement disavowing the hormonal claim specifically. This claim is deliberately **not** included anywhere in this tree. If this material is revisited, keep the distinction clear: "posture affects how you feel" (weakly supported) is not the same claim as "posture changes your hormones" (not supported).
